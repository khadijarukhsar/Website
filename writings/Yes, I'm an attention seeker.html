Yes, I'm an attention seeker

Yes, I'm an attention seeker. And I'm not ashamed of it. Life feels different when you have suicidal ideation. September is the month of suicide prevention. And it's going to end now. I must share what I learned along the way. Maybe that will help someone to fulfill the goal. The first time I listened to the words "attention seeker", I just knew that they were negative. That they were meant to insult someone. I tried restricting myself from exhibiting any behavior that could be called attention-seeking behavior. When a person is trying to hide a certain thing. To survive as best he can. He tries to follow every probable clue in his way. He tries giving up everything that can lead to it. This year I felt suicidal ideation to the next level. Previously, I just knew that these were only the thoughts. And I won't actually do it. But this year, at moments it just felt more intense. Like it was not in my hands anymore. I just wanted to get rid of everything. At once. I have supportive friends to share struggles like these. But I couldn't do that anymore. Whenever I reached out for sharing, I got two types of responses. Either they reciprocated those thoughts. With them being the doer. As if trying to tell me that this kind of thinking of mine hurts them. I think they assumed that I share such thoughts on purpose. Just to test their patience and torture them. Even if it wasn't true, they still did the same. To make me realize how hurtful I can be at times. Coping with this wasn't easy. However, letting this behavior go became easier somehow when I realized something. That they don't deserve to suffer because of me. They don't have the mental capacity to deal with it. I should not burden them. I understand that witnessing someone struggling can be very hard on their loved ones. But here's the thing. If you truly care about people struggling with these things. If you are willing, learn to deal with this situation in the best way possible. Otherwise, please know that it can only make them feel more negative. Within themselves and their close relationships. Even if it was intended to help them. Trust me, it does not. And if you can't provide this safe place for them, it's your responsibility to help them. To find one that would be safe enough. That won't convert every catharsis into a failure. That won't belittle or neglect their call for help. It's wise and kind sometimes. To understand your unique human ability. That you can't be enough supportive for someone. (Especially when the required support is this serious.) The best thing you can do then is to realise your shortcomings. To work on them if you can. And if you can't, accept it and try to compensate it in the best way possible. Second kind of response that I mostly got was attention. Concerned, sincere, caring attention. I felt safe. But my mind turned this into a weapon too. Every time I went through an episode. Every time I wanted to reach out for help. I was told by my mind that I'm doing this for attention. I was an attention seeker. It was an insult. It felt pathetic. Searching about this feeling online I came across an explanation. Explaining attention seeking when suffering that changed my whole view of looking at it. The crux of that explanation was: Feeling like an attention seeker often stops us from doing things we want or need to do. There's nothing wrong with seeking attention. It just means that you need attention. And it is human. These were just ordinary words. But I was in shock to realize that I seek it because I need it. I need it to stay alive. Maybe it's not that bad to be an attention seeker. Maybe attention seeking is the act that will save me at the end. And if I'm safe by doing something why should I care how stigmatized that thing is. In the society or maybe in my own mind. The guilt of getting into the spirals of self harm or suicidal ideation just to get attention, care or politeness of someone was not there anymore. I seek it because I need it and I deserve it. I should consider who is emotionally capable enough to listen to my negative thoughts. But I shouldn't be ashamed of getting attention when my whole life depends on it. This experience made me flexible about other attention seeking behaviours too. No one knows how much a person needs what they are seeking. Extents can be healthy or unhealthy. But even unhealthy extents don't need to be insulted or ridiculed in this matter. The last thing a person with right conscience wants to do is to make circumstances worse. For a human being who is already suffering. The world needs empathy. It needs understanding before judgement. It needs flexibility before ridicule. This change in attitude is necessary among us if we really want to prevent suicides. Or other mental health struggles. So if you really want to help prevent suicides, try being empathetic. About the need for careful attention. And be more open. To accepting your shortcomings. Stay connected effectively. Stay safe. This too shall pass.
