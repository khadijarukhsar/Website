When Creativity and Growth Aligns

When Creativity and Growth Aligns (From the lens of a psychology student.) This entry talks about something that I've been avoiding for a long time. I don't want to follow any rules for the things that give me any recreational experience. The whole purpose of doing that task is expressing the fulfillment. Rules seem like noise. But it turns out I don't exist in a void even if I feel like it. People can actually see me and they read what I write. I have been reaching out to others since I decided to get out of my comfort zone a little. So here we go. I don't write for an audience. I just can't. Then why am I supposed to abide by a list of rules while writing something? Why is there a structure that requires me to first introduce the thing I'm going to talk about, then talk about it, and then conclude it? Conclusions! Like seriously! I'm so bad at ending things. I skip farewells. I dread saying a final goodbye. Most of (actually all of) my write-ups are spontaneous bursts. And let me tell you one thing about these bursts. You don't know when they are about to start, you don't know when they will end. You just know one thing for sure. Their visit gave you a collection of words. Words that are reflections of your conscious and subconscious. How do I tell these bursts to be formal? Just a little bit. How can I ask them to first introduce themselves? How can I demand a proper goodbye? I can't. And maybe that's the thing that distinguishes a writer who really writes and someone for whom, it's a momentary expression. I don't write to reach milestones. I don't want to. But it feels illegal to get constructive feedback from someone and not consider it. I always wanted to keep writing as something that happens by me and not something that I do. Maybe I'm afraid that I'll fail to articulate if I start doing it. I enjoy the electric surges that translate themselves into strings of words. I crave the relief after they pass. Insight # 01: In psychology, we refer to this phenomenon as the Flow State. Flow state is a mental state in which a person is intensely focused on enjoying an activity that he forgets everything else. There's no room for tracking time or worrying about something else. The activity feels smooth and natural. It's pretty common during artistic creations. This concept was given by psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi. (MEE-hy cheek-sent-me-high-ee) This is my second post, and let me share with you my third fear. I'm afraid of developing a rigid mindset. I need growth to tell myself that I'm succeeding in life. Therefore imagine how uncomfortable it would be just to keep writing as I write and not considering some protocols. Insight # 02: We can observe the concept of optimal anxiety here. This concept is a part of Yerkes-Dodson Law, which states that our performance is influenced by stress/anxiety we feel. Less stress makes us feel bored, whereas too much stress makes us feel overwhelmed and anxious. Both affect our performance negatively. However, there's a middle ground. The right amount of stress leads to growth and transformation. This right amount of discomfort is called optimal anxiety. So staying in my comfort zone will not push me to improve my performance. Even if performance is not my goal, growth is. For growth I don't have to feel and do everything at once. It'll keep me stuck and overwhelmed. Just a little stress, that'll make me keep moving. And I'll be on a balanced path. I'm not defining my success and value in terms of acting on someone's feedback. The feedback is just a tool that stirred something I always avoided to attend. A question that makes it difficult for me to look into the mirror. Am I not exploring my potential and masking it as low-maintenance hobbies? Have I resided myself in a box? Is my writing really liberating me? I know it does. It frees me from distress. It brings me peace. It gives me clarity. I've made this liberation my home and I'm trying to postpone the decision of expanding it. I'm afraid of big homes. They often just become houses giving no sense of belongingness. So this is my fourth fear. I don't want to turn my solace into a task. What if I fail in it? And what if in return, my home abandons me? I'm facing this fear by making a commitment. I'll try the protocols. Not to become bound by them, but using them so see how much I can expand my habitat. Psychological Insight 03: External factors (audience, feedback, rules) often decrease a person's intrinsic motivation to perform a task. An example of this phenomenon is the fear that my relief or satisfaction will be threatened if I get involved in rules and structures. Fulfillment is my intrinsic motivation. If it's threatened, there's a chance I'll stop writing at all. This phenomenon is called the Overjustification effect, when external incentives decrease our intrinsic motivation to do something. I'll be careful. I'll take it in small steps. My intuition says I won't turn back. And if it keeps me going, I'll be successful. I'll be happy that I came out of my comfort zone and did something I have been trying to ignore for a long time. So dear friend, your suggestions are appreciated. Just be gentle while giving them. I'll stop, I'll struggle but I'll start again. I'll build my home again.
